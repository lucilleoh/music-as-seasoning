00 Research Question

How does music shape the way we perceive the taste and flavor of food?

01 Motivation

Why this question?

Music's effect on eating behavior is well-documented and relatively unsurprising—slow tempo leads people to eat more slowly and consume more. The perceptual question is stranger: does the song playing overhead actually change how food tastes?

This question sits at the heart of multisensory perception. Although taste feels mouth-bound, research continues revealing how much of what we perceive in any one modality is shaped by integration across the others. Retronasal olfactionRetronasal OlfactionOdor molecules from food in the mouth travel up the back of the throat to the nose during chewing and swallowing. Lose your sense of smell and most of what you call "taste" disappears—which is why food tastes flat with a head cold or after COVID-induced anosmia. is the clearest case. If something as adjacent as smell does most of the work, something as distal as sound might too.

Restaurants rarely think about music as part of the meal—defaulting to the Billboard Top 100 at uncomfortable volumes, often wildly mismatched with the cuisine. This work asks whether that inattention is actually costing the meal something.

02 Previous Research: Study 1

As Bitter as a Trombone

Crisinel & Spence (2010)  ·  Attention, Perception, & Psychophysics

Background

Earlier crossmodal research showed that people consistently matched taste words—"sweet," "sour," "bitter"—to specific pitches and instruments: sweet to higher pitches and piano-like timbres, bitter to lower pitches and brass. However, it was unclear whether these mappings reflected the tastes themselves or just linguistic and cultural associations with the words.

Q.Do taste–sound correspondences exist for real tastes—not just food names?

Hypotheses
  • If taste–sound correspondences operate independently of language, then sweet & sour will map to high pitch and bitter & umami to low pitch—even with real tastants
  • If hedonic value drives instrument choice, then pleasant tastes will pair with pleasant-sounding instruments (e.g., piano), unpleasant tastes with brass
  • Alternative: if earlier word-based effects were driven by language alone, then the pattern will not replicate when real tastes are used

03 Previous Research: Study 2

The Influence of Music on Taste Perception

Campinho, Sousa, & Mata (2023)  ·  International Journal of Gastronomy and Food Science

Q.Does music pre-selected as "sweet" or "sour" actually change how a real food tastes?

Hypotheses
  • If sweet music (medium-high pitch, consonant, slow, legato) modulates taste perception, then it will enhance the perceived sweetness of the dessert
  • If sour music (high-pitch, fast, dissonant, articulated) modulates taste perception, then it will enhance the perceived sourness of the dessert
  • Alternative: if congruency effects observed with abstract sound clips don't transfer to a naturalistic real-food, real-music context, then music will have no measurable effect on taste perception

Method

  • Food stimuli: passion fruit mousseWhy Passion Fruit Mousse?Chosen for its pronounced combination of sweet and sour notes—letting researchers test the influence of music on both tastes within the same dish.

    Recipe ingredients: frozen passion fruit pulp, gelatin sheets, heavy cream, condensed milk. pH 5.2, soluble solids > 30 °Brix. Served at 8 °C in 20 g portions.    —both sweet & sour
  • Music selected by 5 professional musicians blind to hypothesis:
    • Sweet: Nocturne Op. 9 No. 2 in E♭ major · ChopinThe "Sweet" MusicComposed 1830–32 by Frédéric Chopin (1810–1849). Solo piano. Lyrical, slow, legato; medium-high pitch with consonant harmony—features the literature associates with sweet tastes.

      Volume during the study: 71 dB.

      Volume during the study: 76 dB.

The Asymmetric Finding

Sour music enhanced sourness—as predicted. But sweet music suppressed sourness without enhancing sweetness. Music affected sourness only.

Interpretation

Crossmodal taste modulation can operate through balance and inhibition—not just enhancement.

Proposed mechanisms for the asymmetry:

  • Ceiling effectCeiling EffectThe mousse was already very sweet—soluble solids exceeded the refractometer's measurement limit (> 30 °Brix). Sweetness perception may have been near its maximum, leaving no room for music to push it higher.—mousse already very sweet
  • Dessert-label primingDessert-Label PrimingParticipants knew they were eating a "dessert"—a label that strongly primes the expectation of sweetness (Yeomans et al., 2008; Spence & Piqueras-Fiszman, 2014). That expectation may have been strong enough to override subtler auditory effects on sweetness.—expectation overrides auditory cue
  • HabituationHabituationPleasant, sweet-feeling music is extremely common during food consumption (cafés, restaurants, supermarkets). People may simply be less perceptually sensitive to it in food contexts, while sour, dissonant music—rare in these settings—stands out more.—sweet music is common in food contexts
  • Color congruencyColor CongruencyYellow is culturally associated with sourness (Spence, 2017). A yellow dessert paired with "sour" music creates a multi-sensory congruent condition—visual cue + auditory cue both pointing toward sour—which may have amplified the sour effect.—yellow mousse + "sour" music amplify
Methodological caveat

In the music-selection pre-test, the 5 professional musicians showed much stronger effects—both sweetness and sourness significantly modulated—than the 49 lay participants in the main study. May reflect musical training or awareness of the hypothesis.

04 Questions for Future Research

What's next?

Q1.Does the asymmetric suppression effect generalize beyond sweet/sour?

Campinho et al. only tested the sweet/sour pairing. Replicate the design with a salty-bitter food—e.g., dark chocolate with sea salt—and matching "salty" and "bitter" soundtracks designed using Crisinel & Spence's parameters.

  • Same suppression-without-enhancement pattern → general principle of crossmodal inhibition
  • Different pattern → mechanism is taste-pair-specific

Q2.Low-level pitch–taste correspondence, or higher-level priming?

Crisinel & Spence's data point to low-level mechanisms; Campinho et al. propose multiple higher-level ones (semantic priming, emotional valence, color congruency). Design a 2×2 studyProposed 2×2 DesignCross pitch profile (high vs. low) with emotional valence (sweet-feeling vs. sour-feeling):

  • High pitch + sweet-feeling
  • High pitch + sour-feeling
  • Low pitch + sweet-feeling
  • Low pitch + sour-feeling
crossing pitch profile × emotional valence.

  • Pitch-driven → high-pitch conditions pattern together regardless of valence
  • Valence-driven → sweet-feeling conditions pattern together regardless of pitch
05 Implications for Cognitive Science

Flavor is not in the food.

Nor only in the mouth—it emerges from how the brain weaves together signals across the body's sensory surfaces.

Theoretical

Crossmodal effects can suppress as well as enhance—complicating simple additive models of multisensory binding.

Public Health

"Sonic seasoning" could reduce sugar, salt, or fat content without losing perceived intensity—relevant for diet-related disease.

Clinical

Restore eating pleasure for those with taste loss—long COVID, chemotherapy, aging-related dysgeusia.

Commercial

Tune restaurant ambience to cuisine; design multisensory diningSonic Seasoning in the WildCharles Spence (Oxford) has collaborated with chef Heston Blumenthal on multisensory dining experiences at The Fat Duck—including the famous "Sound of the Sea" dish, served with an iPod playing seashore ambient audio. Diners rate the seafood as fresher and more flavorful with the audio than without.; inform product naming and audio branding via crossmodal correspondences.

Major Theme
Brains & Bodies

This work concerns embodied, multisensory perception—how the brain integrates input across sensory channels (gustatory, auditory, olfactory, visual) into the unified experience of flavor.
